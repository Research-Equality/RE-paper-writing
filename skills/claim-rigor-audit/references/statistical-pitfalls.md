# Statistical Pitfalls

## Frequent Overclaim Patterns

- `p < .05` is treated as proof of practical importance
- `p > .05` is treated as proof of no effect
- many subgroup or secondary analyses are reported without correction
- exploratory analyses are written as if they were confirmatory
- effect sizes and intervals are omitted
- tiny samples are used to support strong conclusions
- predictive models are reported without genuine holdout or validation logic

## Audit Questions

- Are exact p-values, effect sizes, and confidence intervals reported where needed?
- Was sample size justified prospectively?
- Were multiple comparisons or subgroup tests controlled?
- Could the result be a false positive from flexible analysis choices?
- Does the conclusion match the precision of the estimate?

## Rewrite Guidance

- Replace certainty language with calibrated uncertainty when intervals are wide.
- Separate exploratory findings from prespecified primary results.
- If a result is underpowered, write it as tentative rather than definitive.
