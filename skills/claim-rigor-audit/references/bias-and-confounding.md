# Bias and Confounding

## Common Biases to Check

- `selection bias`: does the sample differ systematically from the target population?
- `measurement bias`: are outcomes or predictors measured unreliably or differently across groups?
- `observer or detection bias`: could expectations have shaped assessment?
- `attrition bias`: is dropout uneven or unexplained?
- `reporting bias`: were only favorable outcomes emphasized?
- `publication bias`: is the evidence base itself skewed toward positive findings?

## Confounding Questions

- What variable could plausibly affect both the exposure and outcome?
- Was that variable measured?
- Was it controlled by design, matching, stratification, or adjustment?
- Could unmeasured confounding fully explain the observed association?

## Rewrite Guidance

- If confounding remains plausible, replace causal wording with associational wording.
- If bias is likely but unfixable, name it explicitly in limitations and weaken the conclusion.
- If a reviewer would reasonably ask for a stronger control, do not write as if that control already exists.
